# Natural Complementary Science Perspective on Global Warming:

# A Hypothesis Model of Thermal Runaway, Carbon Sink Collapse, and Ocean Breathing System (OBS) Intervention

## Abstract

Current climate mitigation strategies primarily focus on reducing anthropogenic CO₂ emissions. While emission reduction is essential, this approach may not fully explain the accelerating instability observed in the Earth system.

This hypothesis proposes that global warming is not driven solely by increasing CO₂ emissions, but also by the progressive collapse of natural carbon fixation and carbon absorption systems.

Based on the framework of Natural Complementary Science, this paper presents a conceptual mathematical model describing a fatal positive feedback loop involving:

* Atmospheric CO₂ accumulation
* Heat accumulation
* Ocean thermal inertia
* Extreme weather events
* Ecosystem degradation
* Carbon sink collapse
* Reduced absorption and fixation capacity

The model further proposes the Ocean Breathing System (OBS) as a theoretical intervention capable of disrupting this feedback loop by restoring oceanic metabolic functions and carbon fixation capacity.

---

# 1. Introduction

Climate change discussions have traditionally focused on anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions.

However, the Earth’s climate system is governed not only by emissions but also by its ability to absorb, process, and store carbon.

Natural carbon fixation systems include:

* Forest ecosystems
* Soil microbial communities
* Wetlands
* Marine phytoplankton
* Ocean biological pumps

If these systems deteriorate, atmospheric CO₂ concentration may continue increasing even under aggressive decarbonization policies.

This paper explores the hypothesis that the collapse of carbon fixation systems constitutes a major driver of climate instability and thermal runaway.

---

# 3. Mathematical Hypothesis Framework

Let:

* C(t) = Atmospheric CO₂ concentration
* H(t) = Global heat accumulation
* S(t) = Carbon sink capacity
* E(t) = Anthropogenic emissions
* O(t) = Ocean sink integrity
* X(t) = Ecosystem stability

A simplified conceptual model is:

dC/dt = E(t) − S(t)

dH/dt = αC + βH

dS/dt = −γX − δO

dX/dt = −εH

dO/dt = −ζH

where:

* α, β, γ, δ, ε, ζ > 0

This framework illustrates how warming can reduce sink capacity, which in turn accelerates CO₂ accumulation.

---

# 4. Carbon Sink Collapse as a Critical Variable

Many climate models focus on emissions.

This hypothesis emphasizes a second critical variable:

Carbon Sink Collapse.

Examples include:

* Forest degradation
* Soil microbial decline
* Ocean productivity reduction
* Coral reef collapse
* Marine ecosystem destabilization

The reduction of carbon fixation capacity may act as a force multiplier for climate change.

---

# 5. Ocean Thermal Inertia and Delayed Effects

The oceans store vast quantities of heat.

Even if emissions were immediately reduced, accumulated ocean heat may continue influencing climate systems for decades.

This delayed response introduces:

* Time lag effects
* Super El Niño risks
* Extreme marine heatwaves
* Long-term climate instability

Therefore, mitigation strategies focusing solely on emissions may underestimate future warming momentum.

---

# 6. Ocean Breathing System (OBS) Intervention Hypothesis

The Ocean Breathing System (OBS) is proposed as a natural-complementary intervention.

The concept aims to:

* Enhance deep-ocean oxygen circulation
* Support phytoplankton productivity
* Restore marine carbon fixation
* Improve biological pump efficiency
* Strengthen ocean metabolic functions

Within the model, OBS acts by increasing effective sink capacity:

dS/dt = −γX − δO + κOBS

where κ represents the restoration effect generated by OBS deployment.

---

# 7. Discussion

This framework does not claim definitive proof.

Rather, it provides a hypothesis-driven systems model that highlights an often-overlooked dimension of climate change:

the degradation of the Earth's carbon fixation infrastructure.

The model suggests that climate stabilization may require:

* Emission reduction
* Carbon removal
* Ecosystem restoration
* Ocean metabolism recovery

working simultaneously.

---

# 8. Conclusion

Natural Complementary Science proposes that climate change should be viewed not merely as an emissions problem but as a planetary metabolic imbalance.

The collapse of carbon sinks and fixation systems may constitute a critical feedback mechanism accelerating thermal runaway.

The Ocean Breathing System (OBS) is presented as a theoretical intervention capable of restoring portions of the Earth's natural regulatory capacity.

Further simulation, validation, and interdisciplinary research are required.
